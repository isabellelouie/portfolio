Bonus Bakery: Website Redesign

CONTEXT

Bonus Bakery is a plant-based bakery in Vancouver, BC. My team and I evaluated their website and created an interactive prototype with recommendations to optimize their online ordering process. This project was completed as part of a senior level design evaluation course at Simon Fraser University.

ROLE

As a UX Researcher, I conducted heuristic evaluations, usability testing, and affinity diagramming to identify customers' needs and pain points, which were then addressed in the website redesign.

OUTCOME

Since the completion of this project, Bonus Bakery has implemented several of our recommendations, including a simplified flavour selection process and a more straightforward checkout page.

This project was also featured in the SIAT Fall 2022 Showcase and the 2023 FCAT Undergraduate Conference.

Bonus Bakery

Our Solution

A seamless online ordering experience, backed by user research.

Our Process

The Challenge

Managing multiple ordering channels creates extra work and stress for a small business owner

In our initial meeting, our client explained that orders came from various channels, including their website, Instagram messages, text messages, and recurring wholesale orders.

These channels required the owner to manually enter data into a spreadsheet, send order information to their bakers and delivery drivers, and send order confirmation messages to customers.

The Opportunity

How might we create a robust online experience to streamline orders through a single platform?

With the multi-channel strategy hindering the company's scalability due to its reliance on manual processes, our client expressed an interest in streamlining orders through the website.

However, our client had been receiving complaints that customers were facing multiple issues with the site, interferring with their ability to place orders.

To better understand customers' pain points, our team decided to evaluate the design and structure of the website's online ordering process to identify sources of friction and areas for improvement.

The Plan

Our team used three methods to assess the website: heuristic evaluations, an online customer feedback survey, and think-aloud usability studies.

This provided insight from three perspectives: current Bonus Bakery customers, prospective customers who had never interacted with the company before, and our team of designers.

This approach allowed our team to pinpoint critical usability issues, common pain points, and deeply understand customers' ordering behaviors and preferences. Based on our research, our team then developed a list of recommendations and created a high-fidelity prototype of a new design that would better address customers’ needs.

The Research

Heuristic Evaluation

METHODOLOGY

Guided by Jakob Nielsen’s 10 interaction design principles, I conducted a heuristic evaluation of the Bonus Bakery website, focusing on the process of ordering a single product for delivery and in-store pickup. My complete heuristic evaluation report can be found here.

Walkthrough of the existing Bonus Bakery's website

Three crucial issues were found that significantly impacted the user experience:

ISSUE #1

Cumbersome flavour selection process

Users need to manually type their selected flavours in a text box, with no data validation, increasing the risk of user error when placing orders.

ISSUE #2

Unclear order deadlines

Customers must rely on their memory of the order deadlines from the home page pop up when selecting a delivery date.

ISSUE #3

Lack of error prevention when selecting a delivery date

Users are able to place orders even after the daily deadline has passed.

Customer Survey

METHODOLOGY

To supplement our findings from the heuristic evaluation, our team conducted an online customer survey to gather qualitative feedback on the online ordering process from Bonus Bakery’s existing customers.

49 respondents were recruited through Bonus Bakery's Instagram and Facebook pages.

SURVEY INSIGHT #1

60% of respondents suggested improvements to the flavour selection process

"Selecting flavours for the cookies can be slightly tedious as you need to type in each flavour you want. A dropdown menu for the number of each flavour you want would be helpful." - Participant A, Current Customer

SURVEY INSIGHT #2

50% of respondents were either indifferent towards or did not like the pop-up presentation of the order deadline information

"When looking on the site, I couldn’t find the order deadlines stated anywhere except when it first popped up on the home screen. People may forget after reading or click without reading and not be able to find cut off times when they go to place their order." - Participant B, Current Customer

Usability Testing

METHODOLOGY

To gain a deeper understanding of customers’ interactions with the website, our team conducted four think-aloud usability studies on the existing website. I conducted tests with two participants: one current and one prospective customer.

Participants were given three tasks and were asked to describe their thoughts out loud as they completed them. Each task required participants to identify a product that fulfilled the needs of a given scenario and to go through the ordering process until they reached the checkout page.

Our team then conducted affinity diagramming to organize the qualitative data collected, with four common issues emerging.

KEY ISSUES

STUDY INSIGHT #1

Order deadline and delivery information is not easily accessible

“I wish that somewhere before choosing the selection, it would tell the amount of business days it would take for it to be ready.” – Participant 2, Prospective Customer

Although this information was presented in the initial pop-up on the homepage, the participant had difficulty recalling this information when it came to selecting the delivery day they wanted.

STUDY INSIGHT #2

Inability to sort products

Without the ability to sort or filter items, some participants found the products page to be overwhelming and had difficulty finding the products they needed.

STUDY INSIGHT #3

Lack of allergen and ingredient information

The third and most critical concern was the lack of allergen and ingredient information, with participants emphasizing the importance of knowing ingredient information.

"If I had an allergy, I would either call the bakery to confirm the ingredients or just not order at all." - Participant 2, Prospective Customer

STUDY INSIGHT #4

Visual and graphical inconsistencies

"I remember seeing the serving size on a different page but I can't find it on this page... I'm not sure how many this cake feeds." - Participant 1, Current Customer

While the participant was correct in remembering that some pages provided serving size information, this is not consistently shared across all product pages.

Prototype

Based on the findings from the heuristic evaluation, customer survey and usability studies, our team created the first iteration of an interactive prototype with recommended changes. I was responsible for designing interactions, writing microcopy, and adding functionality to user flows.

Key changes included the addition of static cards on the homepage with delivery and deadline information, product filters, revamped product pages with more photos and information about ingredients and serving sizes, and the relocation of the delivery/pickup selection to the checkout page.

PILOT STUDY

With this initial iteration, I ran a pilot study with a peer to collect initial feedback on the design, spot any prototype bugs, proofread content, and ensure tasks were achievable within a reasonable timeframe. This step allowed me to make refinements to the prototype and study design before engaging real users, valuing and respecting their time during testing.

Our team then conducted affinity diagramming to organize the qualitative data collected, with four common issues emerging.

STUDY INSIGHT #1

“The order deadlines should be shown across all pages. The new format on the home page has the same problem as the original website - once you navigate elsewhere you forget about the info.”

– Pilot Study Participant

STUDY INSIGHT #1

"Sorting is not really a filter, so I think 'Sort By' and 'Filters' should be two separate dropdowns."

– Pilot Study Participant

Reflection

This project provided an excellent opportunity to explore a variety of UX research methodologies and develop my skills in designing and conducting usability studies. Analyzing, synthesizing, and integrating research findings into data-driven design solutions was another key aspect of my learning experience.

One significant takeaway was the critical role of participant selection in ensuring the reliability of insights. Implementing a rigorous participant screening process and balancing participant demographics across user groups enhanced the credibility of our research outcomes.

Our final deliverables were well-received by the client, and several of our recommendations were swiftly integrated post-project completion, validating the impact of our research on the design process.
